About the plant

Tulips are perennial bulbiferous plants in the Tulipa genus, known for their large, brightly colored flowers in shades of red, orange, pink, yellow, or white. They belong to the lily family, Liliaceae, and are closely related to Amana, Erythronium, and Gagea. With around seventy-five species divided among four subgenera, tulips are native to a region stretching from Southern Europe to Central Asia but have been widely cultivated and naturalized since the 17th century.
Tulips typically flower in spring, becoming dormant in summer. They were likely cultivated in Persia from the 10th century, becoming prized flowers and symbols of the Ottomans by the 15th century. While present in Byzantine Constantinople from 1055, tulips gained attention in Northern Europe during the 16th century. Tulip mania in the 17th-century Netherlands saw tulip bulbs become highly valued commodities. Although tulip breaking virus-induced variegation is no longer cultivated, the Rembrandts group represents the closest available specimens. Breeding programs have produced numerous hybrids and cultivars, expanding beyond the original species.
