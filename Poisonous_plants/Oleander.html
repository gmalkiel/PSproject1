Oleander

About the plant

Nerium oleander, commonly known as oleander, is a flowering shrub or small tree native to the Mediterranean region and parts of Asia. Oleander is widely cultivated for its attractive, fragrant flowers and evergreen foliage. Here's some information about Nerium oleander:
Oleander typically grows in a wide range of environments, including coastal areas and arid regions. It is known for its lance-shaped, leathery leaves that are arranged in whorls along the stems. The plant can reach heights of 6 to 20 feet, and its branches are often covered in clusters of showy, funnel-shaped flowers. The flowers of oleander come in various colors, including shades of pink, red, white, and yellow. They are often fragrant and bloom in abundance during the warm months. Despite its ornamental beauty, it's important to note that oleander is highly toxic. The plant contains toxic compounds known as oleandrin and nerioside, which can be harmful if ingested. All parts of the plant, including leaves, stems, flowers, and seeds, are toxic. Ingesting even a small amount of oleander can lead to severe poisoning and, in some cases, can be fatal. Oleander poisoning can cause symptoms such as nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, heart irregularities, and, in extreme cases, can lead to death. It's crucial to exercise caution and keep oleander away from areas where children or pets may have access to it. Due to its toxicity, oleander is not recommended for planting in areas frequented by people or animals.
