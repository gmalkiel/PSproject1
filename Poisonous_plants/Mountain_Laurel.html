Mountain Laurel

About the plant

Kalmia latifolia, commonly known as mountain laurel, is an evergreen shrub native to the eastern United States. Here's some information about Kalmia latifolia: Mountain laurel is a broadleaf evergreen shrub that belongs to the family Ericaceae. It is known for its attractive, glossy leaves and clusters of showy flowers. The plant typically reaches heights of 5 to 15 feet, forming a dense, rounded shape.
The leaves of Kalmia latifolia are elliptical and have a leathery texture. They are dark green and remain on the plant throughout the year, providing visual interest even in winter. The shrub produces clusters of cup-shaped flowers that appear in late spring to early summer. The flowers can be white, pink, or shades of red, and they add to the ornamental appeal of the plant. While mountain laurel is valued for its attractive foliage and flowers, it's important to note that all parts of the plant contain toxins called grayanotoxins. These toxins can be harmful if ingested and may cause symptoms such as nausea, vomiting, and in severe cases, more serious health issues. Caution is advised, especially in locations accessible to children and pets. Mountain laurel is commonly used in landscaping for its aesthetic qualities. It is suitable for woodland gardens, borders, and as a foundation plant. The plant thrives in acidic, well-drained soils and prefers partial to full shade. In addition to its ornamental uses, mountain laurel has cultural significance. It is the state flower of both Connecticut and Pennsylvania in the United States. The plant's name, "Kalmia," honors the Swedish botanist Pehr Kalm. When properly placed and admired from a safe distance, mountain laurel stands as a beautiful and resilient addition to landscapes, contributing to the natural beauty of wooded areas and gardens in its native regions.
